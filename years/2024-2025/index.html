Winter 2024

7 February 2025

The Magic Square of Squares (and Other Ways to Avoid Homework*)

Chris Wolird

Let’s ignore midterms and take a stroll through the mathematical landscape with an infamous puzzle. The Magic Square of Squares asks whether a 3×3 grid can be filled with nine distinct perfect squares so that every row, column, and diagonal sums to the same total. Having already stolen thousands of hours from distracted mathematicians, the puzzle will claim another 45 minutes this Friday. We'll meander from Euler's letters, to Lunar arithmetic, visual proofs, hexagonal tessellations, Pythagorean triples, inversive tri-sums, and birational raycasting -- glimpsing the foothills of Algebraic Geometry along the way. (*Those grad students who have transcended the mortal duty of homework may be assured: this puzzle is also an effective deterrent of research authorship, journal readings, and thesis planning).

31 January 2025

A large deviation theorem for C^1 monotone potentials generated by the doubling map

Nicholas Chiem

A physical phenomenon, Anderson localization, was discovered by Phillip Anderson in 1958, but it was only in the 2000's that Bourgain-Goldstein found that a uniform large deviation theorem combined with positive lyapunov exponents were strong indicators of localization. We will give an introduction to the setting to study this phenomenon, which revolves around the dynamics associated with the eigenvalue equation of the Schrodinger operator. We will then discuss the main goal of the project and ideas of the proof in the setting of iid generated potentials. By the end, we will be able to compare the iid case and the doubling map case to understand the complications one will have to face. We will conclude with future directions if this project were to be completed. This talk is in preparation for my oral exam.

Topological Data Analysis: How to apply topology

David Weed

There is often the question in pure mathematics of when is this ever useful. I hope to provide one example of where very abstract seeming ideas are pivotal to real world scenarios. In particular I will discuss persistent homology in regards to topological data analysis. This talk will be accessible to anyone in the program as all definitions will be provided and no prior knowledge is really needed. (If you have always wondered why topology matters this is a good example to see that.)

In this talk, we'll explore the world of analysis without any of the pesky (ε, δ)-definition of limits and instead in the language of the hyperreals. As a detour, in the talk, we'll also explore the "shadowy" techniques of umbral calculus as yet another formulation of the subject we all know and love.

15 November 2024

The Subdivision of the Orbit Category and the Link Orbit Category (for Z/2Z)

Katherine Muckle

The orbit category and the link orbit category are categories that keep track of the equivariant and isovariant maps, respectively, between the quotient sets of G (when they are viewed as G-sets). In this talk, I will outline the proof of my first tiny result concerning the weak equivalence of the subdivision of the orbit category and the link orbit category for G=Z/2Z. (This is practice for my oral in December, so all critiques and advice are much appreciated!)

08 November 2024

Euler characteristic, a simple but useful number

Luis Palacios

For this talk, I want to cover the Euler Characteristic from its more simple definitions to some applications of it. I will review the topological perspective, and how it appears in other contexts such as differential equations, algebra, geometry, topological data analysis, and even measure theory! The usefulness brings a light on how invariants are useful throughout their simplicity!

Nov 1 2024

Mostow rigidity theorem

Mohammad Vaezimonfared

Consider for a moment this fairy tale scenario: any two Riemannian manifolds with isomorphic fundamental groups are isometric. In this imaginary world, topological invariants will be the same as geometric invariants. Mostow rigidity theorem states that any two hyperbolic manifolds ,of dimension>=3, with isomorphic fundamental groups are isometric. In this talk, I will talk about Gromov’s proof of Moscow rigidity theorem and how to measure volume using homology.

Complex Hyperbolic Structures Arising from SU(2, 1)-Higgs Bundles

Zach Virgilio

In this talk I will outline the background needed to follow the main ideas of the talk I will be giving at the AMS on Saturday, then conclude the final 20 minutes with a practice of my AMS talk. Oct 18 2024

Choose Your Own Adventure!

Chris Grossack

When I was a kid I used to love books with branching storylines, where you could flip to different pages in order to read different possible stories. I hope to give you all the same sense of interactivity by presenting a talk that branches! At the start of the seminar, I'll ask for a vote in order to decide between four possible topics: 1. applications of topos theory, 2. an introduction to condensed mathematics, 3. what are infinity categories, and why should you care?, and 4. topological quantum field theories and you. Depending on the topic, there may be other points in the talk where we will have further votes for further branches, for instance in the topos theory talk we could give applications to either algebra, analysis, or logic. I can't wait to see you there!

Oct 11 2024

Mild Solutions of the Navier-Stokes Equations

Satish Chandran

One way to construct solutions to the Navier-Stokes equations is to treat the nonlinearity as a perturbation of the linear part iteratively. Solutions constructed like this are called mild solutions. In this talk, we'll discuss how mild solutions are formally build and some fun facts about them.
